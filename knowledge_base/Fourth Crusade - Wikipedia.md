Fourth Crusade

(1202–1204) was a

Latin Christian

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin\_Christian

armed expedition called by

Pope Innocent III

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope\_Innocent\_III

. The stated intent of the expedition was to recapture the Muslim-controlled city of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerusalem

, by first defeating the powerful Egyptian

Ayyubid Sultanate

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayyubid\_Sultanate

. However, a sequence of economic and political events culminated in the Crusader army's 1202

siege of Zara

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege\_of\_Zara

and the 1204

sack of Constantinople

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sack\_of\_Constantinople

, rather than the conquest of Egypt as originally planned. This led to the

partition of the Byzantine Empire

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partitio\_terrarum\_imperii\_Romaniae

by the Crusaders and their Venetian allies, leading to a period known as the

Frankokratia

("Rule of the Franks" in Greek).

In 1201, the

Republic of Venice

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic\_of\_Venice

contracted with the Crusader leaders to build a dedicated fleet to transport their invasion force. However, the leaders greatly overestimated the number of soldiers who would embark from Venice, since many sailed from other ports, and the army that appeared could not pay the contracted price. In lieu of payment, the Venetian Doge

Enrico Dandolo

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrico\_Dandolo

proposed that the Crusaders back him in attacking the rebellious city of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zara,\_Dalmatia

(Zadar) on the eastern Adriatic coast. This led in November 1202 to the

siege and sack of Zara

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege\_of\_Zara

, the first attack against a Catholic city by a Catholic Crusader army, despite

Pope Innocent III

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope\_Innocent\_III

's calls for the Crusaders not to attack fellow Christians. The city was then brought under Venetian control. When the Pope heard of this, he temporarily

excommunicated

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excommunicated

the Crusader army.

In January 1203, en route to Jerusalem, the Crusader leadership entered into an agreement with the Byzantine prince

Alexios Angelos

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexios\_IV\_Angelos

to divert their main force to

Constantinople

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople

and restore his deposed father

Isaac II Angelos

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac\_II\_Angelos

as emperor, who would then add his support to their invasion of Jerusalem. On 23 June 1203, the main Crusader army reached Constantinople, while other contingents (perhaps a majority of all crusaders) continued to

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre\_(city)

In August 1203, following the

siege of Constantinople

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege\_of\_Constantinople\_(1203)

, Alexios was crowned co-emperor. However, in January 1204 he was deposed by a popular uprising, depriving the Crusaders of their promised bounty payments. Following the murder of Alexios on 8 February, the Crusaders decided on the outright conquest of the city. In April 1204, they captured and plundered the city's enormous wealth. Only a handful of the Crusaders continued to the Holy Land thereafter. Several prominent Crusaders, including

Enguerrand II of Boves

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enguerrand\_II\_of\_Boves

Simon de Montfort, 5th Earl of Leicester

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon\_de\_Montfort,\_5th\_Earl\_of\_Leicester

Guy of Vaux-de-Cernay

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guy\_of\_Vaux-de-Cernay

, among others, disagreed with the attacks on Zara and Constantinople, refused to take part in them and left the crusade.

The conquest of Constantinople was followed by the fragmentation of the

Byzantine Empire

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine\_Empire

into three states centered in

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire\_of\_Nicaea

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire\_of\_Trebizond

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Despotate\_of\_Epirus

. The Crusaders then founded several new Crusader states, known as

Frankokratia

, in former Roman territory, largely hinged upon the

Latin Empire of Constantinople

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin\_Empire\_of\_Constantinople

. The presence of the Latin Crusader states almost immediately led to war with the

Byzantine successor states

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicaean-Latin\_wars

and with the

Bulgarian Empire

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgarian-Latin\_wars

. The Nicaean Empire eventually

recovered Constantinople

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconquest\_of\_Constantinople

and restored the Byzantine Empire in July 1261.

The Fourth Crusade is considered to have solidified the

East–West Schism

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East%E2%80%93West\_Schism

. The crusade dealt an irrevocable blow to the Byzantine Empire, contributing to

its decline

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decline\_of\_the\_Byzantine\_Empire

and fall as all the unstable governments in the region, the Sack of Constantinople, and the thousands of deaths had left the region depleted of soldiers, resources, people and money, leaving it vulnerable to attack. Additionally, the empire had badly shrunk as it lost control of most of the Balkans, Anatolia, and Aegean islands. This made the restored empire both territorially diminished and vulnerable to

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine%E2%80%93Ottoman\_wars

from the expanding

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman\_Sultanate

in the following centuries, to which the Byzantines ultimately succumbed

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth\_Crusade&action=edit&section=3

Constantinople had been in existence for 874 years at the time of the Fourth Crusade and was the largest and most sophisticated city in Christendom. Almost alone amongst major medieval urban centres, it had retained the civic structures, public baths, forums, monuments, and aqueducts of classical Rome in working form. At its height, the city was home to an estimated population of about half a million people

Reign of Alexios IV

Alexios IV realised that his promises were hard to keep. Alexios III had managed to flee with 1,000 pounds of gold and some priceless jewels, leaving the imperial treasury short on funds. At that point the young emperor ordered the destruction and melting of valuable Roman icons in order to extract their gold and silver, but even then he could only raise 100,000 silver marks. War against Alexios V

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth\_Crusade&action=edit&section=11

Venetian mosaic from 1213 depicting the fall of Constantinople

The crusaders and Venetians, incensed at the murder of their supposed patron, demanded that Mourtzouphlos honour the contract that Alexios IV had promised. When the emperor refused, the Crusaders assaulted the city once again. On 8 April Alexios V's army put up a strong resistance, which did much to discourage the Crusaders. The Byzantines hurled large projectiles onto the enemy siege engines, shattering many of them. Bad weather conditions were a serious hindrance to the crusaders. A fierce wind blew from the shore and prevented most of the ships from drawing close enough to the walls to launch an assault. Only five of the wall's towers were actually engaged and none of these could be secured; by mid-afternoon it was evident that the attack had failed.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth\_Crusade#cite\_note-FOOTNOTEPhillips2004221%E2%80%93257-48

The Latin clergy discussed the situation amongst themselves and settled upon the message they wished to spread through the demoralised army. They had to convince the men that the events of 9 April were not God's judgment on a sinful enterprise: the campaign, they argued, was righteous and with proper belief it would succeed. The concept of God testing the determination of the crusaders through temporary setbacks was a familiar means for the clergy to explain failure in the course of a campaign. The clergy's message was designed to reassure and encourage the Crusaders. Their argument that the attack on Constantinople was spiritual revolved around two themes. First, the Greeks were traitors and murderers since they had killed their rightful lord, Alexios IV. The churchmen used inflammatory language and claimed that "the Greeks were worse than the Jews",

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth\_Crusade#cite\_note-FOOTNOTEPhillips2004221%E2%80%93257-48

and they invoked the authority of God and the pope to take action.

Although Innocent III had again demanded that they not attack, the papal letter was suppressed by the clergy, and the crusaders prepared for their own attack, while the Venetians attacked from the sea. Alexios V's army stayed in the city to fight, but when the unpaid Varangians left the city, Alexios V himself fled during the night. An attempt was made to find a further replacement emperor from amongst the Byzantine Greek nobility, but the situation had now become too chaotic for either of the two candidates who came forward to find sufficient support.

A strong northern wind aided the Venetian ships in coming close to the walls and after a short battle approximately seventy crusaders managed to enter the city. Some were able to knock holes in the walls, large enough for only a few knights at a time to crawl through; the Venetians were also successful at scaling the walls from the sea, though there was fighting with the Byzantine infantry. The remaining Anglo-Saxon "axe bearers" had been amongst the most effective of the city's defenders, but they now attempted to negotiate higher wages from their Byzantine employers, before dispersing or surrendering.
