What was the Spanish Treasure Fleet?

Spanish Treasure Fleet

consisted of ships from Spain bringing goods and supplies to their New World colonies and then returning home with treasure and natural resources. Two convoys annually partook in this lengthy voyage, stopping at several ports where they unloaded and sold their cargo. At the same time, they added new cargo, including tons of silver melted into ingots, destined to fill the royal coffers of the Spanish crown.

Creation of the Spanish Treasure Fleet

The creation of the Spanish treasure fleet came during a time in which Europe was hungry for precious metals. Spain was especially eager to obtain a reliable source of gold and silver to mint coins. Coins, at the time, were increasing in use as a form of monetary exchange, but were inconsistent in purity. Spain wanted to increase the purity, and thus the value of their coinage. After Christopher Columbus stumbled upon the New World in 1492, Spain quickly took action to colonize the region and exploit its natural resources.

In 1520, the first Spanish treasure fleets began to sail between Spain and the New World. However, it was not until 1543 that Spain devised its two-fleet system. Two major convoys departed from the Spanish port of Seville. One left in the spring, and was called the

New Spain Fleet

, while the other, the

Tierra Firma Fleet

sailed in late summer. Both usually returned together after wintering in the New World.

Because the voyage was long and dangerous, a new type of ship called a Spanish

, was introduced in the 1550s. Galleons sat high in the water, making them difficult for enemies to scale their sides, and had two to three decks, three to four masts, and a sizable cargo hold. They were also well-armed with cannons. The Spanish also sometimes used smaller, swifter ships to carry treasure, that could outrun potential threats from privateers and pirates.

For additional protection, the convoys were escorted by warships. The escorts were paid for their protection with taxes gathered from the sales of goods to merchants and colonists.

Spanish galleons were introduced in the 1550s

Routes of the Spanish Fleet

After taking leave of Seville, Spain, the New Spain Fleet sailed across the Atlantic Ocean. The convoy would stop in the Canary Islands to resupply, before continuing on to the New World. Veracruz, Mexico was the destination and the last port of call before heading to Havana, Cuba. The Tierra Firma Fleet was destined for the ports of Cartegena, Colombia, and Portobello, Panama.

The Tierra Firma Fleet carried more treasure than the New Spain Fleet and included tons of silver, which was mined in Potosi, Peru, melted into silver ingots or bullion, and minted into

. The silver was then transported to Portobello where it would be loaded onto the ships.

A third treasure fleet, the Manila Galleons, brought goods from Asia. These goods included silks and porcelains which were loaded on the Manila Galleons at the port of Manilla in the Philippines. These ships then sailed to Acapulco on the Pacific coast of New Spain (Mexico). These luxury items were then transported on a land route to the port of Veracruz, on the eastern coast of New Spain. The New Spain Fleet would then receive the cargo and take it back with them to Spain.

The Spanish Fleet and the Tierra Firma Fleet wintered in the Spanish colonies. In the spring they would meet up in Havana Harbor, Cuba, from which they would sail home to Spain.

Attacks and Wrecks of the Spanish Fleet

The Spanish Fleet faced many perils. Privateers preyed on the ships and hurricanes sank entire convoys. Conflicts with other countries who sought to diminish Spanish power and acquire its riches also took its toll on the convoys as did hurricanes.

Privateers and the Spanish Treasure Fleet

In one particular incident, in 1628, the Dutch, who had been fighting against Spain for decades, sent

, a privateer, to the Caribbean in command of numerous ships to capture Spanish treasure ships. Heyn was able to take the Spanish off-guard when part of his shipping entourage returned to the Netherlands, thus making them feel safe to set sail for Spain. However, this was a ruse, for the main portion of Heyn's ships had remained behind and laid in wait. They captured the entire fleet, which was heavy with gold and silver, in a battle at Matanzas Bay, Cuba.

Dutch privateer Piet Hein capturing a Spanish treasure fleet near Cuba

Pirates also posed a danger during this period. However, they were more interested in capturing single ships; convoys were not worth the risk. Convoys were well-armed and capable of defending themselves against pirate attacks.

Although pirates and privateers were a very real threat, it was the weather that posed the most danger for the Spanish treasure fleets. It is estimated that Spain lost nearly 700 ships between the latter part of the 15th century and the end of the 19th century. Not all these ships were treasure ships, but the exact number of them that sank is difficult to ascertain.

Some of the Spanish treasure was contemporaneously recovered, but the wrecks of some of these richly laden ships were not found until the 20th century, with the aid of technology. Even with technology, however, there are many ships that remain lost, their treasures waiting to be found.

One of the most famous treasure fleet wrecks occurred in 1715 when a hurricane caught Spanish treasure ships on their way home to Seville, Spain. The ships were wrecked off the southern coast of Florida and it is estimated that 1,000 people died in the disaster. There were, however, survivors, who were able to recount the story of what happened. Only one ship, the

Urca de Lima

remained. It was damaged but the supplies on board were salvageable and aided in the survival of the people who did make it to shore.

In 1733, yet another hurricane struck, wrecking most of a Spanish treasure convoy off the Florida Keys. Again, there were survivors, and because many of the ships did not completely sink, they were able to scavenge for supplies while they waited for rescue.

Decline of the Spanish Treasure Fleet

The latter part of the 17th century witnessed the decline of the Spanish treasure fleets. Spain had been at war for many years with the French, Dutch, and English, and this had taken a toll on Spain's economy, and its mines in the New World were playing out. Additionally, the countries that Spain had been fighting with were establishing their own colonies in the region, thus upsetting Spain's monopoly on its resources and on trade.

Another problem Spain faced was that Spanish colonists began buying goods from other European countries, including products being smuggled into the colonies because they were more affordable. It was no longer cost-efficient for Spain to continue its Spanish treasure fleet system, and by the late 18th century, it had run its course.

Timeline of the Spanish Treasure Fleet Voyages

Spanish treasure fleets sailed the seas beginning in the 16th century. The following is a recap of notable dates in the timeline of Spanish treasure fleet voyages.

In 1520, the first Spanish treasure fleets begin their transatlantic voyages from Spain to the New World and back to Spain

1543 saw the organization of the treasure fleets into two convoys that sailed during the spring and summer at separate times but returned to Spain together

In the 1550s, the Spanish galleon was introduced

1628, the Dutch privateer Piet Heyn captures a Spanish treasure fleet near Cuba

1715 Spanish treasure fleet wrecked off of Florida

1733 Spanish treasure fleet wrecked off of the Florida Keys

1778 witnessed the end of the Spanish treasure fleet system

Lesson Summary

Spanish treasure fleet

was a fleet of ships that sailed out of Spain for the New World bringing the colonists desirable and needed European goods and then returning to Spain with riches and natural resources. The Spanish first set sail in the early 16th century, but became an organized fleet system circa 1543, with two major convoys, protected by warships, sailing twice a year from Seville, Spain. These convoys were called the

treasure ships or

Tierra Firma

or 'galleones

. The major ports these ships visited were Veracruz, in New Spain (Mexico) and Cartagena, Columbia, and Portobello, Panama.

Spanish treasure fleets heading home to Spain faced many challenges, including privateers and violent storms. In 1628, an entire fleet was captured by Dutch privateer

near Cuba. In 1715 and 1730, respectively, Spanish treasure galleons were wrecked off the coast of Florida and the Florida Keys when hurricanes brutally battered the ship, sinking some, and wrecking others, the latter of which were scavenged for supplies by survivors. It is estimated that between the 15th and late 19th century Spain lost around 700 ships. Many remain missing to this day. The Spanish treasure fleet system began to decline in the late 1600s as wars with numerous European countries impacted Spain's economy, its silver mines in the New World became less productive, and other European countries began to establish colonies in the New World. Additionally, because it was more affordable for Spanish colonists to buy goods from smugglers and other European countries, Spain lost out on tax revenue. By 1778, the Spanish fleet system no longer existed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happened to the Spanish treasure fleet?

The Spanish treasure fleets were eventually discontinued. Spain was no longer as powerful as it once was. It was suffering economically after engaging in numerous conflicts, the mines in its colonies were running out of silver, and it no longer monopolized the resources of the New World as other countries began colonies in the region.

How many Spanish treasure ships sunk?

The exact number of Spanish treasure ships sunk is unknown. However, it is estimated that nearly 700 ships were sunk or wrecked between the 15th and late 19th centuries.
