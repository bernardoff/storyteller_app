Medieval demography

is the study of human

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demography

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe

and the Mediterranean during the

Middle Ages

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle\_Ages

. It estimates and seeks to explain the number of people who were alive during the Medieval period, population trends, life expectancy, family structure, and related issues. Demography is considered a crucial element of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History

change throughout the Middle Ages.

The population of Europe remained at a low level in the Early Middle Ages, boomed during the High Middle Ages and reached a peak around 1300, then a number of calamities caused a steep decline, the nature of which historians have debated. Population levels began to recover around the late 15th century, gaining momentum in the early 16th century.

The science of medieval demography relies on various lines of evidence, such as administrative records, wills and other types of records, archaeological field data, economic data, and written histories. Because the data are often incomplete and/or ambiguous, there can be significant disagreement among medieval demographers.

Demographic history of Europe

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medieval\_demography&action=edit&section=1

The population levels of Europe during the Middle Ages can be roughly categorized:

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

400–600 (Late Antiquity): population decline

600–1000 (Early Middle Ages): stable at a low level, with intermittent growth.

1000–1250 (High Middle Ages): population boom and expansion.

1250–1348 (Late Middle Ages): stable or intermittently rising at a high level,

with fall in 1315–17

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great\_Famine\_of\_1315%E2%80%931317

in most of Europe.

1348–1420 (Late Middle Ages): steep decline in England and France, growth in East Central Europe.

1420–1470 (Late Middle Ages): stable or intermittently falling to a low level in Western Europe, growth in East Central Europe.

1470–onward: slow expansion gaining momentum in the early 16th century.

Late Antiquity

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late\_Antiquity

saw various indicators of Roman civilization beginning to decline, including urbanization, seaborne commerce, and total population. Only 40% as many Mediterranean shipwrecks have been found for the 3rd century as for the 1st.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-2

During the period from 150 to 400, with the intermittent appearance of plague, the

population of the Roman Empire

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics\_of\_the\_Roman\_Empire

ranged from a high of 70 to a low of 50 million, followed by a fairly good recovery if not to the previous highs of the Early Empire. Serious gradual depopulation began in the West only in the 5th century and in the East due to the appearance of bubonic plague in 541 after 250 years of economic growth after the troubles which afflicted the empire from the 250s to 270s. Proximate causes of the population decrease include the

Antonine Plague

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonine\_Plague

(165–180), the

Plague of Cyprian

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plague\_of\_Cyprian

Crisis of the Third Century

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis\_of\_the\_Third\_Century

. European population probably reached a minimum during the

extreme weather events of 535–536

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme\_weather\_events\_of\_535%E2%80%93536

and the ensuing

Plague of Justinian

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plague\_of\_Justinian

(541–542). Some have connected this demographic transition to the

Migration Period Pessimum

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2000\_Year\_Temperature\_Comparison.png

clarificationneeded

when a decrease in global temperatures impaired agricultural yields.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-3

Early Middle Ages

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early\_Middle\_Ages

Viking expansion

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking\_expansion

Arab expansion

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slav

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian\_people

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

This rural, uncertain life spurred the development of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism

Christianization

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianization

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Estimates of the total population of Europe are speculative, but at the time of

Charlemagne

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne

it is thought to have been between 25 and 30 million, of which perhaps half were in the

Carolingian

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian

Empire that covered modern France, the Low Countries, western Germany, Austria, Slovenia, northern Italy and part of northern Spain.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Most medieval settlements remained small, with agricultural land and large zones of unpopulated and lawless

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilderness

in between (Geographers estimate that around 800, as much as three-quarters of Europe was still forested).

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Population density was only two to five persons per square kilometer in Britain, similarly in Germany, and somewhat higher in France.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-4

Manorial surveys and some allusions to provincial hearth taxes suggest a population of 5 million for Carolingian France. Presumed densities of settlement support estimates of 4 million for Italy and a similar number for Iberia, as well as German lands (including Scandinavia); 6 million for Slavic lands and perhaps 2 million for Greece and southern Balkans; 1.5 million people for the entire British Isles.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-New\_York\_:\_Charles\_Scribner's\_Sons-5

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-bartlett-6

High Middle Ages

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High\_Middle\_Ages

, many forests and marshes were cleared and cultivated.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-bartlett-6

At the same time, during the

Ostsiedlung

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostsiedlung

, Germans resettled east of the

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elbe

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saale

Polabian Slavs

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polabian\_Slavs

Crusaders expanded to the

Crusader states

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusader\_states

, parts of the

Iberian Peninsula

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberian\_Peninsula

reconquered from the Moors

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconquista

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normans

colonized England and

southern Italy

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern\_Italy

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-bartlett-6

These movements and conquests are part of a larger pattern of population expansion and resettlement that occurred in Europe at this time.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-bartlett-6

Reasons for this expansion and colonization include an improving climate known as the

Medieval warm period

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_warm\_period

, which resulted in longer and more productive growing seasons; the end of the raids by

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikings

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early\_Muslim\_conquests

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian\_invasions\_of\_Europe

, resulting in greater political stability; advancements in

medieval technology

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_technology

allowing more land to be farmed; 11th-century reforms of the Church that further increased social stability; and the rise of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism

, which also brought a measure of social stability.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Towns and trade revived, and the rise of a money economy began to weaken the bonds of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serfdom

that tied peasants to the land.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Land was at first plentiful while labour to clear and work the land was scarce; lords who owned the land found new ways to attract and keep labour.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Urban centres were able to attract serfs with the promise of freedom.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

As new regions were settled, both internally and externally, population naturally increased.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Overall, European population tripled between the years 1000 to 1348

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-7

and is estimated to have reached a peak of 73.5 million to as high as 100 million,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

which is substantially higher than the population of the Roman Empire at its peak.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Seidel,\_Walter\_2007-8

– The population of England, between 1.25 and 2 million in 1086,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-9

is estimated to have grown to somewhere between 3.7 million

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Russell,\_Josiah\_Cox\_1972.\_p.\_122-10

and 5–7 million,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

although the 14th-century estimates derive from sources after the first plague epidemics, and the estimates for pre-plague population depends on assumed plague mortality, the proportion of children and the rate of omissions in returns of taxable population.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Russell,\_Josiah\_Cox\_1972.\_p.\_122-10

Germany/Scandinavia

– The population in Germany and Scandinavia rose from 4 million in 1000, to 11.5 million by 1340.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

– Italy's population around 1300 has been variously estimated at between 10 and 13 million. The two largest cities in Italy, Venice and Florence, had about 100,000 persons each. The larger cities constituted as high as twenty percent of Italy's population.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-11

By 1300, the population of the entire province of Tuscany may have then surpassed 2 million people — a level the region would not reach again until after 1850.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-New\_York\_:\_Charles\_Scribner's\_Sons-5

– Danish population reached a peak of 1 million by 13th century, estimated from a survey partially preserved in Waldemar's Land Book (1231).

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-12

– In 1328, France is believed to have supported between 15 and 17 million people (in a smaller geographical area than today's) and 20 million people (in the present-day area),

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-13

the latter not reached again until the

early modern period

Kingdom of Hungary

The extent of destruction is reflected in low population growth in the subsequent period. Even in the early 14th century the population was only slightly higher, between 1.4 and 2.3 million.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-16

Late Middle Ages

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medieval\_demography&action=edit&section=5

Citizens of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tournai

By the 14th century, the frontiers of settled cultivation had ceased to expand and internal colonization was coming to an end, but population levels remained high. Then a series of events—sometimes called the

Crisis of the Late Middle Ages

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis\_of\_the\_Late\_Middle\_Ages

—collectively killed millions. Starting with the

Great Famine

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great\_Famine\_of\_1315%E2%80%931317

in 1315 and the

Black Death

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black\_Death

from 1348, the population of Europe fell abruptly. The period between 1348 and 1420 saw the heaviest loss. In parts of Germany, about 40% of the named inhabitants disappeared.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

The population of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Provence

was reportedly halved and in some parts of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuscany

, 70% were lost during this period.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Historians have struggled to explain why so many died.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Some have questioned the long-standing theory that the decline in population was caused only by infectious disease (see further discussions at

Black Death

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black\_Death

) and so historians have examined other social factors, as follows.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malthusian

argument has been put forward that Europe was

overpopulated

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human\_overpopulation

: even in good times it was barely able to feed its population.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Grain yields in the 14th century were between 2:1 and 7:1 (2:1 means for every seed planted, 2 are harvested.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Modern grain yields are 30:1 or more.)

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Malnutrition developed gradually over decades, lowering resistance to disease, and competition for resources meant more warfare, and then finally crop yields were pushed down by the

Little Ice Age

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little\_Ice\_Age

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

An alternative theory is that competition for resources exacerbated the imbalance between property-owners and workers,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

and that the money supply ceased to keep up with fixed increased economic activity (being

commodity money

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodity\_money

based principally on silver)

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

so that wages sank while rents rose,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

leading to demographic stagnation. The economic conditions of the poor also aggravated the calamities of the plague because they had no recourse, such as fleeing to a villa in the country in the manner of the nobles in the

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

The poor lived in crowded conditions and could not isolate the sick, and had weaker immunities from a deficient diet, difficult living and working conditions and poor sanitation.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

After the plague and other exogenous causes of population decline lowered the labor supply, wages increased.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

This increased the mobility of labour and led to a

redistribution of wealth

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redistribution\_of\_wealth

, although property-owners' attempts to resist change through wage freezes and price controls

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

contributed to

popular uprisings

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular\_revolt\_in\_late\_medieval\_Europe

such as the

Peasants' Revolt

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasants%27\_Revolt

of 1381. By 1450, the total population of Europe was substantially below that of 150 years earlier, but all classes overall had a higher standard of living.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

The Brenner Debate

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medieval\_demography&action=edit&section=6

Another theory, as introduced by

Robert Paul Brenner

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert\_Brenner

in a 1976 paper, is that the economic system of the High Middle Ages limited population growth.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Brenner-24

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-25

Feudal lords

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overlord

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landlord

controlled most of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe

's land; they could charge high enough rents or demand a large enough percentage of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peasant

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profit\_margin

that peasants on these lands were forced to survive at

subsistence levels

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsistence\_economy

. With any surplus of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food

, labor, and

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Income

absorbed by the landowners, the peasants did not have enough

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital\_(economics)

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment\_(economics)

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farm

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incentive

to increase the

productivity

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Productivity

of their land.

In addition, the small size of most peasants' farms inhibited centralized and more efficient cultivation of land on larger fields. In regions of Europe where

primogeniture

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primogeniture

was less widely practiced, peasant lands were subdivided and re-subdivided with each generation of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inheritance

; Brenner writes that consequently: "This too naturally reduced the general level of peasant income, the surplus available for potential investment in agriculture, and the slim hope of agricultural innovation."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Brenner-24

As a result, according to Brenner, on account of the social and economic system, the size of Europe's population was limited; the

existing agricultural system

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture\_in\_the\_Middle\_Ages

medieval technology

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_technology

could not support a population beyond a certain size. When the population of Europe surpassed the threshold that the existing economic structure permitted: population loss, social instability, and famine could result. Only through modifying the existing social structure of land ownership and distribution could Europe's population surpass early 14th century levels.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-Brenner-24

Regardless of the cause, populations continued to fall into the 15th century and remained low into the 16th because the plague returned in cycles over the course of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, although subsequent plagues, such as the "children's plagues" of the 1360s were less virulent than the Great Plague of 1347–1348.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-26

Science and art of medieval demography

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medieval\_demography&action=edit&section=7

Sources traditionally used by modern demographers, such as marriage, birth and death records, are often not available for this period, so scholars rely on other sources, such as archaeological surveys, and written records when available.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Examples of field data include the physical size of a settlement, and how it grows over time, and the

appearance, or disappearance, of settlements

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abandoned\_village

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

For example, after the

Black Death

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black\_Death

the archaeological record shows the abandonment of upwards of 25% of all villages in

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

However, archaeological data are often difficult to interpret.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

It is often difficult to assign a precise age to discoveries. Also, some of the largest and most important sites are still occupied and cannot be investigated.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Available archaeological records may be concentrated on the more peripheral regions, for example early Middle Ages

Anglo–Saxon

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Saxons

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutton\_Hoo

, in East Anglia in England, for which otherwise no records exist.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Because of these limitations, much of our knowledge comes from written records: descriptive and administrative accounts. Descriptive accounts include those of chroniclers who wrote about the size of armies, victims of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List\_of\_wars

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Famine

, participants in an oath. However these cannot be relied on as accurate, and are most useful as supporting evidence rather than being taken factually on their own.

The most important written accounts are those contained in administrative records.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

These accounts are more objective and accurate because the motivation for writing them was not to influence others.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

These records can be divided into two categories: surveys and serial documents. Surveys cover an estate or region on a particular date, rather like a modern inventory.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manorial

surveys were very common throughout the Middle Ages, in particular in France and England, but faded as serfdom gave way to a money economy.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Fiscal surveys came with the rise of the money economy, the most famous and earliest being the

Domesday Book

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domesday\_Book

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

The Book of Hearths from Italy in 1244 is another example. The largest fiscal survey was of France in 1328. As kings continued to look for new ways to raise money, these fiscal surveys increased in number and scope over time. Surveys have limitations, because they are only a snapshot in time; they do not show long-term trends, and they tend to exclude elements of society.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Serial records come in different forms.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

The earliest are from the 8th century and are land conveyances, such as sales, exchanges, donations, and leases.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1

Other types of serial records include death records from religious institutions and baptismal registrations. Other helpful records include

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heriot

, court records,

food prices

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food\_prices

and rent prices, from which inferences can be made.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval\_demography#cite\_note-numbers-1
