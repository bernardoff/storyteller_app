The Spanish Main

The Spanish Main was the name given to the mainland coast around the Caribbean Sea. This region became part of the

Spanish Empire

https://www.q-files.com/history/latin-america/history-of-latin-america

from the 16th century. From here, a fabulous amount of goods were shipped to Spain by the treasure fleet. Goods included gold, silver, gemstones, spices, hardwoods and animal hides. The Spanish Main was a magnet for pirates and privateers, so Spain guarded the fleet with war galleons.

Treasure and coins

The native peoples of Central and South America made beautifully crafted ornaments, statues and jewellery from precious metals. The Spanish melted down most of the gold and silver they seized from native peoples and turned it into coins or ingots. Their historical value was lost forever. 
 Gold coins were called “doubloons”. Silver coins were called “pesos”.

The fleet sails through Caribbean waters

Nueva España Flota

Nueva España Flota

sailed in April to the port of Veracruz in Mexico (called Nueva España, or New Spain). Another fleet, known as the Manila Galleons, crossed the Pacific Ocean from the Philippines bringing goods such as porcelain, silks, ivory and spices (as well as enslaved Filipinos) from China and Japan to Acapulco, on the Pacific coast of Mexico. From here, mule trains shipped the goods overland to Veracruz. The yearly arrival at Acapulco of the Manila Galleons drew pirates to the Pacific coast of Mexico. The galleons, sometimes more than 50 metres (165 feet) long, were the largest ships in the Spanish fleet.

Nueva España Flota

collected both the Manila Galleons cargo and silver from the Zacatecas mines and overwintered in Veracruz. In the spring, it made the three-week journey to Havana on the island of Cuba. There the ships underwent repairs and took on cargoes of tobacco and sugar.

Tierra Firme Flota

Silver from Potosí travelled on the second of Spain's Indies

. Llama trains carried the silver mined in Potosí across the Andes Mountains and the Atacama Desert to Arica (in present-day Chile) on the Pacific coast of South America. Ships of the South Seas Fleet transported the cargo as far as Panama City. It was then hauled overland across the isthmus (a narrow neck of land) to Porto Bello, an 80-kilometre (50-mile) trek known as the Spanish Silver Train—and a prime target for English, Dutch and French privateers.

At Porto Bello the silver was collected by ships of the

Tierra Firme Flota

which had arrived from the port of Cartagena, and returned there to spend the winter. In the spring, the

Tierra Firme Flota

sailed for Havana to rendezvous with the

Nueva España Flota

Royal galleons

The royal galleons leave harbour

The galleons escorting the treasure fleet formed what was known as the

Armada de la Guardia

("Guard Fleet"). They left the fleet on arriving in the Caribbean and sailed on to Havana, from where they returned to Spain with the previous year’s fleet. This meant they did not have to spend winter in the region. These ships, known as the royal galleons, were originally escort man-of-wars, but later transported the most valuable treasures in their own holds.
