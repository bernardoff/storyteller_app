Golden Age of Piracy

was the period between the 1650s and the 1730s, when maritime

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy

was a significant factor in the histories of the North

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic

Indian Oceans

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian\_Ocean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-DelawareDate-1

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-2

Histories of piracy often subdivide the Golden Age of Piracy into three periods:

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-Dateline-3

buccaneering

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buccaneer

period (approximately 1650 to 1680), characterized by Anglo-French seamen based in

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martinique

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tortuga\_(Haiti)

Spanish colonies

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish\_colonies

, and shipping in the

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean

and eastern

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific

to western Pacific.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-Dateline-3

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-4

Pirate Round

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pirate\_Round

(1690s), associated with long-distance voyages from various Caribbean and North American ports to established bases in countries like

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar

, in order to rob Muslim and

East India Company

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East\_India\_Company

targets in the

Indian Ocean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian\_Ocean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red\_Sea

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-Dateline-3

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-5

The post-Spanish Succession period (1715 to 1730), when English sailors and

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privateers

left unemployed by the end of the

War of the Spanish Succession

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War\_of\_the\_Spanish\_Succession

turned en masse to

piracy in the Caribbean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy\_in\_the\_Caribbean

, the Indian Ocean, the

North American

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North\_America

eastern seaboard, and the

West African

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West\_Africa

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-Dateline-3

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-6

Narrower definitions of the Golden Age sometimes exclude the first or second periods, but most include at least some portion of the third. The modern conception of pirates as depicted in popular culture is derived largely, although not always accurately, from the Golden Age of Piracy.

Factors contributing to piracy during the Golden Age included the rise in quantities of valuable cargoes being shipped to Europe over vast ocean areas, reduced European navies in certain regions, the training and experience that many sailors had gained in European navies (particularly the British

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal\_Navy

), and corrupt and ineffective government in European overseas colonies.

Colonial powers

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonialism

at the time constantly fought with pirates and engaged in several notable battles and other related events.

Name of the Golden Age

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=1

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=2

Amaro Pargo

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amaro\_Pargo

, a Spaniard who was one of the most famous corsairs of the Golden Age of Piracy

The oldest known literary mention of a "Golden Age" of piracy is from 1894, when the English

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journalist

George Powell wrote about "What appears to have been the golden age of piracy up to the last decade of the 17th century."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-7

Powell uses the phrase while reviewing

Charles Leslie

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles\_Leslie\_(writer)

A New and Exact History of Jamaica

, then over 150 years old. Powell uses the phrase only once.

In 1897, a more systematic use of the phrase "Golden Age of Piracy" was introduced by historian

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John\_Fiske\_(philosopher)

, who wrote, "At no other time in the world's history has the business of piracy thriven so greatly as in the seventeenth century and the first part of the eighteenth. Its golden age may be said to have extended from about 1650 to about 1720."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-8

Fiske included the activities of the

Barbary corsairs

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbary\_corsairs

and East Asian pirates in this "Golden Age," noting that "as these Mussulman pirates and those of Eastern Asia were as busily at work in the seventeenth century as at any other time, their case does not impair my statement that the age of the buccaneers was the Golden Age of piracy."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-9

Pirate historians of the first half of the 20th century occasionally adopted Fiske's term "Golden Age," without necessarily following his beginning and ending dates for it.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-10

The most expansive definition of an age of piracy was that of Patrick Pringle, who wrote in 1951 that "the most flourishing era in the history of piracy ... began in the reign of

Queen Elizabeth I

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queen\_Elizabeth\_I

and ended in the second decade of the eighteenth century."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-11

This idea starkly contradicted Fiske, who had hotly denied that such Elizabethan figures as

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis\_Drake

were pirates.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-12

Trend toward narrow definitions

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=3

Of recent definitions, that given by Pringle appears to have the widest range, an exception to an overall trend among historians from 1909 until the 1990s, toward narrowing the Golden Age.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-13

As early as 1924, Philip Gosse described piracy as being at its height "from 1680 until 1730." In his highly popular 1978 book

The Pirates

for TimeLife's

The Seafarers

series, Douglas Botting defined the Golden Age as lasting "barely 30 years, starting at the close of the 17th Century and ending in the first quarter of the 18th."

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-14

Botting's definition was closely followed by Frank Sherry in 1986.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-15

In a 1989 academic article, Professor

Marcus Rediker

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcus\_Rediker

defined the Golden Age as lasting only from 1716 to 1726.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-16

Angus Konstam

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angus\_Konstam

in 1998, reckoned the era as lasting from 1700 until 1730.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-17

Perhaps the ultimate step in restricting the Golden Age was in Konstam's 2005

The History of Pirates,

in which he retreated from his own earlier definition, called a 1690–1730 definition of the Golden Age "generous," and concluded that "The worst of these pirate excesses was limited to an eight-year period, from 1714 until 1722, so the true Golden Age cannot even be called a 'golden decade.'"

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-18

Recent countertrend toward broader meaning

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=4

David Cordingly

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David\_Cordingly

, in his influential 1994 work

Under the Black Flag

, defined the "great age of piracy" as lasting from the 1650s to around 1725, very close to Fiske's definition of the Golden Age.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-19

Rediker, in 2004, described the most complex definition of the Golden Age to date. He proposes a "golden age of piracy, which spanned the period from roughly 1650 to 1730," which he subdivides into three distinct "generations": the buccaneers of 1650–1680, the Indian Ocean pirates of the 1690s, and the pirates of the years 1716–1726.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-20

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=5

#### Main article:

Piracy in the Caribbean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piracy\_in\_the\_Caribbean

Piracy arose out of, and mirrored on a smaller scale, conflicts over trade and colonization among the rival European powers of the time, including the empires of Britain, Spain, the Netherlands, Portugal, and France. Most pirates in this era were of English, Welsh, Dutch, Irish, and French origin. Many pirates came from poorer urban areas, such as the ports of London, in search of a way to make money and of reprieve.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-History\_Pirate-21

Piracy, therefore, "offered power and quick riches".

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-History\_Pirate-21

Buccaneering period, c. 1650–1680

https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy&action=edit&section=6

#### Main article:

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buccaneer

Historians such as John Fiske mark the beginning of the Golden Age of Piracy at around 1650, when the end of the

Wars of Religion

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European\_wars\_of\_religion

allowed European countries to resume the development of their colonial empires. This involved considerable seaborne trade and a general economic improvement: there was money to be made – or stolen – and much of it traveled by ship.

French buccaneers had established themselves on northern

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispaniola

as early as 1625,

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-22

but lived at first mostly as hunters of pigs and cattle rather than robbers; their transition to full-time piracy was gradual and motivated in part by Spanish efforts to wipe out both the buccaneers and the prey animals on which they depended. The buccaneers' migration from Hispaniola's mainland to the more defensible offshore island of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tortuga\_(Haiti)

limited their resources and accelerated their piratical raids. According to

Alexandre Exquemelin

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandre\_Exquemelin

, a buccaneer and historian from this period, the Tortuga buccaneer

Pierre Le Grand

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre\_le\_Grand\_(pirate)

pioneered the settlers' attacks on

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galleons

making the return voyage to Spain. The French buccaneer

François l'Olonnais

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois\_l%27Olonnais

Jean-David Nau

) was known for his extreme infamous cruelty towards Spanish prisoners on the island of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martinique

served as a home port for French buccaneers like him, allowing the French to operate in the Caribbean against Spanish ships.

The growth of buccaneering on Tortuga was augmented by the English capture of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamaica

from Spain in 1655. The early English governors of Jamaica freely granted

letters of marque

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letters\_of\_marque

to Tortuga buccaneers and to their own countrymen, while the growth of

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port\_Royal

Transatlantic shipping traffic between Africa, the Caribbean, and Europe began to soar in the 18th century, a model known as the

triangular trade

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangular\_trade

, and became a rich target for piracy. Trade ships sailed from Europe to the African coast, trading manufactured goods and weapons for slaves. The traders then sailed to the Caribbean to sell the slaves, and return to Europe with goods such as sugar, tobacco, and cocoa. In another triangular trade route, ships carried raw materials, preserved cod, and rum to Europe, where a portion of the cargo was sold for manufactured goods, which (along with the remainder of the original load) were then transported to the Caribbean, where they were exchanged for sugar and molasses, which (with some manufactured articles) were then borne to New England. Ships in the Triangular Trade often made money at each stop.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-33

This arrangement also contributed heavily to the spread of piracy across the western Atlantic. Shipping to the colonies boomed along with the flood of skilled mariners after the war. Merchant shippers used the surplus of labor to drive wages down, cut corners to maximize profits, and create unsavory conditions aboard their vessels. Merchant sailors suffered from mortality rates as high or higher than the slaves being transported.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-34

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-History\_Pirate-21

The increased volume of shipping traffic also could sustain a large body of brigands preying upon it.

During this time, many of the pirates had originally been either sailors for the Royal Navy, privateersmen, or merchant seamen.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-History\_Pirate-21

Most pirates had experience living on the sea, and knew how harsh the conditions could be. Sailors for the king often had very little to eat while out on the sea, and ended up sick, starving, and dying. That resulted in some sailors deserting the king and becoming pirates instead. Compared to merchant vessels, crewmembers on pirate ships often had as much power as the captain outside of battle. The captain only had full authority in times of battle and could be removed from this position if he showed cowardice in the face of the enemy.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden\_Age\_of\_Piracy#cite\_note-35

He was also to be bold in battle, and pirates did not want things to end up the same way as on a navy ship.
